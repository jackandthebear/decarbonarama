Which EV Charger Should You Buy?

Most people overpay or buy the wrong charger because nobody explains the choices in order. Answer a few questions below and you'll land on one of three clear setups — A, B or C — with the reasons why.

Decarbonarma · Interactive guide · Updated July 2026 · ~7-min read

Going electric is really two decisions, not one. First: should you get an EV at all, and how do you pay for it? Second — the bit people rush — how will you charge it? Get the charging decision wrong and you either overspend on kit you don't need, or you save £400 up front and then lose far more paying premium public-charging rates for years. This guide walks you through both, in the right order.

First, the car: buy, lease or finance?

You don't need to own an EV outright to benefit. The three common routes each suit different people:

Personal Contract Purchase (PCP) keeps monthly payments low by deferring a big "balloon" payment to the end — you then hand the car back, pay the balloon to keep it, or roll into a new deal. It suits people who like changing cars every 3–4 years and want to sidestep the risk of where used-EV values land. Personal Contract Hire (leasing) is pure rental: fixed monthly cost, hand it back at the end, no resale worry — often the cheapest way into a newer EV, and salary-sacrifice schemes through an employer can make it dramatically cheaper still by taking payments before tax. Buying outright or on a loan (HP) costs most up front but least overall, and the car is yours.

Whichever route you pick, the running-cost case is the same and it's strong: charged at home on a cheap overnight tariff, an EV can cost around 2–3p per mile in electricity versus roughly 13–16p per mile in petrol. That gap is exactly why the charging decision below matters so much — it's where the savings live.

The one thing that changes everything

Do you have off-street parking — a driveway, garage or allocated bay where you can run a cable to the car? If yes, home charging is open to you and it's transformative. If no, you'll lean on public charging, and the maths is very different. The tool below starts there.

Recommendation · Type B

Standard 7 kW home charger

A fitted 7 kW smart charger on your normal electricity tariff (or a simple Economy 7 night rate). You get fast, convenient home charging without changing supplier — you can always add a smart EV tariff later to unlock the cheapest rates.

  • Cost to run: your standard day rate (~25–30p/kWh), or cheaper on an Economy 7 overnight window.
  • Speed: same fast ~25–30 miles per hour as Type A — the charger hardware is the same.
  • Needs: off-street parking. No tariff switch required.
  • Upgrade path: the charger is already "smart", so moving to Intelligent Octopus Go later is just a tariff change — no new kit.
Recommendation · Type C

"Granny" charger (3-pin, 2.3 kW)

A portable cable that plugs into a normal 13A socket. Cheap up front (often supplied with the car) and fine if you drive very little or need a stop-gap — but slow, and not a good everyday solution for higher mileage.

  • Cost to buy: low — no installation needed. Charges on whatever your household rate is.
  • Speed: only ~8 miles of range per hour — a near-empty battery can take 24 hours+ to fill.
  • Watch out: use a dedicated outdoor-rated socket on its own circuit; don't run it off extension leads. As a portable device it sits outside the smart-charger rules.
  • Better later: if your mileage climbs, upgrade to a fitted 7 kW unit (Type A or B).
Recommendation · No driveway

Public charging (+ a granny cable as backup)

Without off-street parking you can't fit a home charger, so you'll rely on the public network — rapid chargers for quick top-ups and slower "destination" chargers at supermarkets, gyms and workplaces.

  • Cost to run: higher — public rapid charging is typically 45–80p/kWh, so the home-charging savings mostly don't apply. Factor this into whether an EV works for you yet.
  • Grant help: from April 2026, renters and flat owners can get up to £500 per socket toward a charger if they later gain a private space or via on-street schemes — worth checking your council's plans.
  • Backup: keep a granny cable for the rare occasion you can reach a normal socket.
  • Tip: a workplace or nearby destination charger you use regularly can replace much of the home-charging benefit.

Before you buy a fitted charger

Use an OZEV-approved installer, and if you're a renter or flat owner check the £500-per-socket grant first — you must be approved before installation. Homeowners with a private driveway no longer qualify for that residential grant, but the running-cost savings from a smart tariff pay a 7 kW charger back quickly on their own.

The short version: if you've got a driveway and drive a normal amount, fit a 7 kW smart charger — Type A if you'll switch to a smart tariff, Type B if not (and you can upgrade later). Only drop to a granny charger if your mileage is genuinely tiny, and lean on public charging if you can't park off-street. Buy for the setup you'll actually use, and the wrong-charger trap disappears.
